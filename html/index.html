Let's take a look at the data


Total Cases in all States and Regions graphed against Activity Levels and Weeks of the Year

Take note of the top-right quadrant, where we have Activity Level plotted against Week. Here we see the number of cases start off high at the beginning of the year, then drop off, only to then rise again by the beginning of the year. The raw number cases seem to be highest during the cold winter months of the year, and decrease as the months get warmer.

Total Cases grouped by Severity Levels, graphed against Activity Levels and Weeks of the Year

Take note, again, of the top-right graph. Here we see a similar pattern of total cases going up in the cold months. What we also see is that more cases tend to be severe in the winter months, while the summer months appear to show mostly Minimal to Moderate cases.


Total Cases grouped by Flu Seasons, graphed against Activity Levels and Weeks of the Year

We see a similar pattern to the previous graphs, where cases are fewer in the Summer months. However, we see that for the 2023-2024 season, there is a greater number of “Minimal Cases” in December, and a greater number of “Very High” cases in January. This may be an indicator of a more active flu season. Note: The date of this report is Feb 7, 2024, so the data for the 2023-2024 Flu Season is incomplete.

Total Number of Cases in each of the ten States and Regions with the highest total number of cases

Notice how the numbers are fairly flat. This actually holds true for all states and regions, as none have a number of cases that drop below 65. This is likely misleading, as it seems unlikely all regions would have roughly the same number of cases. Investigation into the collections methods for this dataset shows that due to gaps in reporting and data collection, the dataset is highly normalized. It is meant to accurately reflect the severity of cases across time during a flu season, NOT the total number of cases in a geographical region, nor how widespread those cases are within a given region.


Top Ten States from the graph above, but number of cases are color coded by Severity Level

Upon cursory glance, there may appear to be some loose correlation between population size and/or density and the number of severe outbreaks. However, a more complete and rigorous analysis of the data is needed to know conclusively.

Top Ten States and Regions with severe cases

Take note of the two largest entries on the left. These are not states, but cities. New York City and the District of Columbia. This seems to suggest that there is a likely strong correlation with population density and the severity of respiratory illness outbreaks.


A bar graph of all 52 Weeks of the Year and the number of severe case outbreaks in all regions

Here we can confirm what many medical professionals and laypersons know intuitively: Flu gets worse in the cold. However, with enough data collection and analysis, we are well on our way towards a more quantified predictive model that prove invaluable for planning and distributing medical resources.

Possible Solutions

Now that our data has been cleaned, processed and analyzed, let's see how we can use it to shape the future

Expand public transportation in densly populated cities
Solution 1

It is no surprise that air pollution can pose a threat to your respiratory health. We suspect that an an increase of public transoportation availability can help curb the rise in ILIs caused by air polution.

Expand research on ILI causing viruses that thrive in cold weather
Solution 2

We noticed that a large majority of severe cases arise during the cold months. To help address this issue, we suggest expanding research on the pathogens that are more likely to survive in lower temperatures.

Promote sanitation practices in highly populated areas
Solution 3

In the more populated ares, air quality is not the only concern. In dense cities, it is very easy to pass contagions from one person to the next. Promoting more strict sanitary practices in these areas can decrease the liklihood that someone will pass on ILIs to those around them.
